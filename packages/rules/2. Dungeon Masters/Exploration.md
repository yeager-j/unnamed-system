## Creating a Game

When you sit down behind the screen, you are more than a referee or a storyteller. You are a game designer working live at the table. Every scene you describe, every option you offer, and every consequence you adjudicate shapes what kind of game your players are playing. This section offers guidance on running scenes that feel like a game, not a movie.

### The Game Designer's Mindset

It is tempting to divide a session into two modes: combat, where the rules apply, and roleplay, where they don't. Resist this division. Roleplay is not a break from gameplay; it *is* gameplay. Your players should feel like they are playing a game in the marketplace, in the war council, and at the dinner table just as much as they do when swords are drawn.

A scene becomes a game the moment players face multiple, non-arbitrary options and must weigh risk against reward. If a player's only choices are "do the thing the plot wants" or "do something the DM will punish," they aren't playing a game — they're watching one. Your job, scene by scene, is to make sure a real decision is always in front of them.

### Highlighting Interactables

In a video game, the player knows they can interact with an object because it has a glowing outline, a floating prompt, or a button hint on screen. At the table, your descriptions serve the same purpose. The objects you name are the objects your players will reach for; everything else fades into the background.

Aim to highlight two to four objects in any given scene. One feels like a railroad; the players know you are pointing at the only "real" choice. Five or more buries them; they'll latch onto whatever they remember last and forget the rest. Two to four gives them a meaningful spread without becoming overwhelming.

Once you've described the scene, wait. Don't ask, "What do you do?" Let the players bite on the objects you placed in front of them.

> [!warning] Instead of...
> **Player:** I open the door.
> 
> **DM:** As you push the door open, it reveals a massive library with tall bookshelves lining the walls.
> 
> **Player:** *(Uncomfortable silence)* Uh, okay? I look for an exit.

> [!success] Consider
> **Player:** I open the door.
> 
> **DM:** As you push the door open, it reveals a massive library with tall bookshelves lining the walls. A cracked skylight filters in gentle rays of dusty sunlight. You notice a bookshelf just twenty feet away with a series of scrolls. In the center of the room, there's a table with a strange arrangement of candles.
> 
> **Player:** I check out the scrolls!
> 
> **Another Player:** I want to see what's up with those candles.

Be careful about creating Interactables that aren't... well, interactable. This can lead to player confusion and make them feel like they're missing something. It may also lead to players ignoring future Interactables because they assume it'll be nothing again.

That being said, an Interactable doesn't have to **do** anything in particular. It can also help tell a story. When creating Interactables like these, make sure they grant useful knowledge or help them connect threads you've laid out already.

> [!warning] Instead of...
> **DM:** You enter into the witch-queen's bedroom. Moonlight flows in from the large windows in the back illuminating a portrait.
> 
> **Player:** I investigate the portrait.
> 
> **DM:** It depicts the queen before she became a witch.
> 
> **Player:** Uh, is there anything behind the portrait?
> 
> **DM:** No

> [!success] Consider
> **DM:** You enter into the witch-queen's bedroom. Moonlight flows in from the large windows in the back illuminating a portrait.
> 
> **Player:** I investigate the portrait.
> 
> **DM:** It depicts the queen as she was years ago before she became a witch. She's holding a small, sickly child in her arms. The queen is smiling, but there is pain behind her eyes.
> 
> **Player:** Ohhh, maybe she became a witch to save her kid?

### The Exploration Loop

Exploration runs on a simple cycle: information leads to interaction, and interaction reveals more information. A player hears about a strange table of candles, walks over to inspect it, and discovers a hidden compartment beneath it. That discovery prompts another round of investigation. Keep the loop turning, and the scene will sustain itself.

Three habits keep the loop healthy.

**Serve information in small portions.** A wall of text is hard to track and harder to act on. Describe just enough to give the players something to grab, then stop.

**Describe, don't interpret.** Tell the players what is there, not what it means. A cracked skylight is interesting because it might let someone climb out, or because it might let something climb in—that is a discovery for the players to make. The moment you flag an object as "useful," you've answered the question for them.

**Keep your initial descriptions short.** A short description invites clarifying questions. "There's a strange arrangement of candles on the table" is an invitation. Once they've accepted your invitation, feel free to "render" the scene further: "There's an arrangement of seven black candles in a six-pointed star, with melted wax pooling beneath each one and a faint sulfurous smell rising from the center. Thin wisps of smoke rise from each candle." When rendering a scene, make sure the details are useful as discussed in the previous section. The player might infer that a ritual was conducted *very recently*, which could be useful information.

### Nesting and Branching Scenes

Once players begin interacting with an object, you can introduce new highlights one layer down. A drawer is a single interactable; once opened, it might reveal a wand, a velvet pouch, and a bundle of papers. This *nesting* lets you give a scene real depth without dumping everything on the players at once.

When a scene branches—a fork in the road, three doors in a hallway, a choice of which suspect to interrogate first—give each branch a distinct identity. A path is not a real choice if the players have no way to evaluate it. "You can go left, right, or forward" is a coin flip. "You hear movement from the left, smell something rotten from the middle, and see total darkness ahead" is a game.

> [!warning] Instead of...
> **DM:** You enter the cave system and, after a few minutes of walking, come upon a junction. You can proceed to the left, to the right, or continue forward.
> 
> **Player:** Uh, the middle I guess?

> [!success] Consider
> **DM:** You enter the cave system and, after a few minutes of walking, come upon a junction. You hear noises from the left path, like the movement of some creature. The middle path has a distinct smell of rot, and you can see bones littering the ground. Finally, the right path is dead quiet and dark, as if it's empty.
> 
> **Barbarian:** Let's go kill whatever's making those noises!
> 
> **Rogue:** What? Why get ourselves into trouble? Let's try the dark path!
> 
> **Bard:** I don’t care what we do as long as it doesn’t involve the smelly gross middle path.

When a room genuinely has nothing of interest, say so. If your players spend ten minutes searching an empty broom closet, you've trained them to distrust your descriptions—they'll start assuming everything you mention has a hidden secret. Tell them the room is bare. If they search anyway, don't call for a roll; tell them they find nothing and move on.

> [!info] When to Call for a Roll
> A Focus Test is appropriate only when there is something to find *and* finding it is uncertain. If the answer is "definitely yes" or "definitely no," skip the dice and narrate the result.

### Applying the Loop to Other Pillars

The information-interaction cycle isn't only for dungeons. Any time the players seem stalled or disengaged, ask whether you've given them a real game to play.

**Downtime.** Players often go silent when offered free time because they don't know what the options are. Instead of asking "What do you want to do this week?", present three or four specific, competing activities: train at the dojo to raise a Virtue, work a shift at the café to earn gold, attend a noble's gala to make a contact, or investigate the rumors about the missing children. A choice between concrete things is a game, but a game without choices is a cutscene.

**Social Encounters.** A consequential conversation deserves the same structure as a dungeon. Set the stakes, the rewards, and the difficulties before it begins. The players should know what's on the table and what they stand to lose if they push too hard. Without those stakes, they're just chatting. See [[2.3 Social Interaction#Negotiation]] for more information on this.

**Loot and Economy.** Gold becomes meaningful only when there are competing things to spend it on. A potion of healing, a custom-forged blade, a bribe to spring an ally from prison, a donation to fund the orphanage—each of these is a small game in itself. If the only thing to spend gold on is "the next better sword," you might as well skip the gold and give them the sword in the first place.
