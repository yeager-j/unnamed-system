## How to use this toolkit

Each entry below is an NPC blueprint built from a single Major Arcana. Draw or choose a card and you get three things: a personality with its built-in failure mode (the "at their best / at their worst" axis), a ready-made character arc (the direction the story wants to pull them), and two or three worked examples to show the range. The arcana describes the _soul_ of the NPC, not their occupation — a Hermit can be a hacker, a wandering monk, or a reclusive noble; what makes them a Hermit is the shape of their wound and their growth.

Read the first paragraph of each entry for the archetype and the second for how to play it. The examples are from the Persona games that inspired this toolkit and are there to spark variations, not to be copied.

## Major Arcana

### I. The Magician

The Magician stands with one arm raised to the heavens and one pointing to the earth, channeling raw will into the world. Upright, the card means manifestation, resourcefulness, inspired action, and the power to make things happen; reversed, it warns of manipulation, self-deception, scattered energy, and talent spent on the wrong things. The Magician archetype is the extrovert whose outward confidence and appetite for life run well ahead of any real self-knowledge — the instigator, the enthusiast, the one whose energy gets everyone moving before anyone has thought it through.

At their best, Magician characters are loyal, warm, generous with their time, and the emotional engine of any group they belong to; at their worst they are self-absorbed, immature, performatively confident, and quietly lying to themselves about what they actually want. The arc bends from raw enthusiasm toward genuine self-knowledge. Kenji Tomochika chases a fantasy romance with a teacher and must face whether he loved her or only the idea of being the kind of person who dates one. Yosuke Hanamura hides grief and small-town frustration behind goofiness and learns to own a real feeling. Morgana is outwardly arrogant, but consumed by anxiety over his own uncertain identity, desperate to prove he counts as human. Play the Magician as someone whose desire outruns their wisdom — and whose growth is the slow arrival of self-awareness underneath all that noise.

### II. The Priestess

The High Priestess sits veiled between two pillars, a half-hidden scroll in her lap and the moon at her feet — intuition, the subconscious, inner wisdom, and knowledge not yet spoken aloud. Reversed, she signals a disconnection from that inner voice: repressed feeling, secrets kept too long, and a retreat into the self. The Priestess archetype is the brilliant, composed, somewhat reserved figure whose evident competence sits on top of a private, unvoiced self-doubt.

At their best, Priestess characters are perceptive, gentle, disciplined, and deeply capable; at their worst they are passive, self-effacing, and inclined to let others define who they are rather than risk their own judgment. The arc runs from a borrowed or imposed identity toward an inner voice they finally learn to trust. Fuuka Yamagishi, shy and once badly bullied, discovers through trial and error that she has a worth and a skill that are genuinely her own. Yukiko Amagi, serene and admired on the surface but privately waiting for a prince to carry her off, stops looking to be rescued and realizes the want is her own — that she can choose the inn rather than simply being sentenced to it. Makoto Niijima, the rule-following overachiever, stops outsourcing her decisions to authority and expectation and learns to act on her own read of a situation. Play the Priestess as someone whose wisdom is real but unclaimed, and whose growth is daring to speak it.

### III. The Empress

The Empress sits enthroned amid flowering abundance — the great mother of the deck, embodying fertility, creativity, nurturing, and the generative power that lets things grow. Reversed, she curdles into smothering control, dependence, or a creative and emotional emptiness behind the bounty. The Empress archetype lives in the gap between a regal, maternal presentation and the person underneath it — often someone of wealth, rank, or station who is quietly starved of ordinary human warmth.

At their best, Empress characters are dignified, caring, refined, and devoted to cultivating something or someone beyond themselves; at their worst they are cold, controlling, isolated by their position, or self-sacrificing to the point of vanishing. The arc frees a high-born figure from inherited obligation into genuine self-determination. Mitsuru Kirijo, an heiress and leader who has never been allowed an ordinary pleasure, explores small everyday joys and ultimately refuses an arranged marriage to choose her own path. Haru Okumura, the gentle heiress to a food empire, tends her rooftop garden and her dream of a quiet café while escaping the marriage and corporate fate her father arranged for her. Play the Empress as someone whose graciousness is real but whose life has been scripted by others — and whose growth is taking the pen.

### IV. The Emperor

The Emperor sits armored on a stone throne carved with rams' heads — established authority, structure, discipline, and the imposition of order on chaos. Reversed, he becomes tyranny and rigidity, or its mirror image: someone who cannot exert control at all and bullies to hide it. The Emperor archetype is about authority and the structures we build to feel secure, and the strongest versions interrogate that authority rather than simply wield it.

At their best, Emperor characters are principled, strong-willed, protective, and committed to building something orderly and fair; at their worst they are domineering, self-righteous, rigid, and so afraid of powerlessness that they tyrannize others to feel in control. The arc bends toward redefining what authority — and strength itself — should mean. Hidetoshi Odagiri, a zealous disciplinarian, nearly ruins innocent people chasing a culprit before realizing that exclusion and punishment are not the same as justice, his hunger for control masking a fear of being made the scapegoat. Kanji Tatsumi, feared as a brute, is really a soft-hearted boy ashamed of his love of sewing and delicate things, and his growth is accepting both halves of himself. Yusuke Kitagawa must topple a corrupt mentor who posed as his master to build an identity founded on truth instead of borrowed authority. Play the Emperor as someone who confuses dominance with strength — and whose growth is learning the difference.

### V. The Hierophant

The Hierophant — the high priest — sits between two pillars blessing his acolytes: tradition, shared values, mentorship, and the wisdom handed down across generations. Reversed, he is dogma and rigid conformity, or the necessary challenge to a tradition that has outlived its use. The Hierophant archetype is the older mentor or guardian a generation removed from the young, the keeper of accumulated life-wisdom and the weight of the past.

At their best, Hierophant characters are warm, grounded, wise, and quietly generous keepers of home and tradition; at their worst they are stubborn, gruff, emotionally walled-off, or trapped clinging to what they have already lost. The arc reconciles an older person with grief, with family, or with a guardian role they have struggled to fill. Bunkichi and Mitsuko, an elderly bookshop couple, make peace with the death of their son, a grief embodied in a memorial tree the local school wants to cut down — and conclude that helping the living matters more than guarding the past. Ryotaro Dojima learns to grieve his late wife and become a real father to the daughter he has been neglecting. Sojiro Sakura, gruff and guarded, reveals the tender reason he took in an orphaned girl. Play the Hierophant as someone carrying an old wound beneath their wisdom — and whose growth is finally embracing the family in front of them.

### VI. The Lovers

The Lovers shows two figures beneath a blessing angel, and the card is as much about _choice_ and the alignment of values as it is about romance — union, harmony, and decisions made from the heart. Reversed, it is disharmony, misalignment, bad choices, and the war between heart and head. The Lovers archetype is the warm, attractive, emotionally central figure who is too easily reduced to an object of others' desire or projection, and whose real task is becoming a whole person first.

At their best, Lovers characters are warm, supportive, open-hearted, and magnetic; at their worst they are insecure, dependent on approval, and unsure who they are beneath the image others want from them. The arc strips away a mask of expectation to reveal an authentic, self-chosen self. Yukari Takeba works through grief and abandonment over her dead father before she can let anyone close. Rise Kujikawa, a burnt-out idol, struggles to reconcile her manufactured public persona with the real girl underneath, finally accepting that both are genuinely her. Ann Takamaki, forever dismissed as a pretty face, turns guilt over a friend's suffering into self-respect and resolve. Play the Lovers as someone the world keeps flattening into a symbol — and whose growth is insisting on being a person who chooses.

### VII. The Chariot

The Chariot shows a warrior driving two sphinxes forward by will alone — victory, determination, momentum, and the mastery of opposing forces through sheer drive. Reversed, it is loss of control, aimless aggression, and wheels spinning with nowhere to go. The Chariot archetype is the most physical and forward-charging of the deck: hot-blooded, loud, brave, and usually running faster than they are steering.

At their best, Chariot characters are energetic, loyal, courageous, and relentless in pursuit of a goal; at their worst they are reckless, hot-tempered, insecure beneath the bravado, and prone to barreling ahead without direction. The arc converts raw drive into self-aware strength. Kazushi Miyamoto pushes through an injury that threatens his athletic dreams and has to learn where determination ends and self-destruction begins. Chie Satonaka confronts a hidden jealousy of her best friend and redefines strength as the will to protect rather than to dominate. Ryuji Sakamoto, an ex-track star with a wrecked knee and a ruined reputation, reclaims his pride and rebuilds the bond with the teammates he thought he'd lost. Play the Chariot as someone with more momentum than aim — and whose growth is learning where to point it.

### VIII. Justice

Justice holds the sword and the scales — truth, cause and effect, accountability, and the weighing of right against wrong until balance is restored. Reversed, it is dishonesty, unfairness, the evasion of accountability, and retribution dressed up as fairness. The Justice archetype looks wildly different on the surface from one character to the next, but they share one engine: a relationship to moral _principle_ that runs ahead of the messy reality around them. They hold themselves to a high standard and expect the same of everyone else, and their arc is the collision between that standard and a world that refuses to balance cleanly.

At their best, Justice characters are earnest, principled, honest, and quietly brave in defense of what is right; at their worst they are rigid, self-righteous, and trapped in a black-and-white view that can curdle, at its darkest, into vengeance mistaken for fairness. The defining image is the sword and the scales: the immature Justice figure reaches only for the sword — clean verdicts, good people and bad people — while the arc, when it succeeds, teaches them to hold the scales, to weigh and to grant nuance. Chihiro Fushimi, falsely suspected over missing funds, must find the courage to defend the truth despite her fear. Nanako Dojima, a child asking why bad people are bad, learns the world is greyer than her storybook morality without losing the care underneath it. Goro Akechi is the cautionary endpoint — a boy whose collision with a profoundly unjust reality shatters his scales entirely, leaving only a sword swung in the name of personal vengeance. Play the Justice NPC as a dial as much as an archetype: an idealist meeting reality, who can come out with their integrity intact, their innocence matured, or their principles perverted into something monstrous.

**Don't confuse with —** the **Devil** and **Temperance**. Justice *values* a principle openly and reaches for the verdict; the Devil hides a chosen obsession behind a noble-sounding reason; Temperance is torn between two genuine goods rather than holding one absolute standard. If "truth" is a value they'd pass judgment for, it's Justice — if it's a cover for something they won't look at, it's the Devil.

### IX. The Hermit

The Hermit stands alone on a peak, holding up a lantern — solitude, introspection, and the inward search for meaning, wisdom earned by stepping away from the world. Reversed, it is isolation taken too far: loneliness, withdrawal, and losing one's way in the dark. The Hermit archetype is the person who has cut themselves off from human contact, hiding behind a screen, a shell, or a fortress of solitude they built for protection and can no longer leave.

At their best, Hermit characters are insightful, self-sufficient, perceptive, and quietly profound; at their worst they are reclusive, socially fearful, lonely, and walled off from the connection they secretly need. The arc draws a hidden person gently back into the world. Maya exists to the protagonist only as an anonymous voice through a computer, the whole friendship conducted at a remove that slowly reveals the lonely adult behind the screen. Futaba Sakura, an agoraphobic shut-in crushed by guilt over her mother's death, takes halting steps back into a world she had sealed herself away from. Play the Hermit as someone whose solitude began as a refuge and became a prison — and whose growth is the courage to come back down the mountain.

### X. Wheel of Fortune

The Wheel of Fortune turns endlessly — fate, cycles, chance, and the sudden reversals that lift people up or cast them down regardless of their wishes. Reversed, it is bad luck, resistance to change, and the struggle against a current that seems fixed. The Fortune archetype is varied in temperament but unified by one predicament: they are all dealing with the hand fate dealt them. Birth, parentage, gender, or raw talent set them on a path — and it is not the path they would have chosen. Their arc is the struggle against that inheritance and, when it succeeds, the act of seizing authorship of their own life.

At their best, Fortune characters are determined, self-aware, and quietly courageous in claiming a future that is genuinely theirs; at their worst they are fatalistic, trapped, or resigned to a role assigned to them by circumstances they never agreed to. Keisuke Hiraga is fated to become a doctor by a family of doctors and a domineering father — and when that father tries to seize control of even his artistic life, Keisuke finally pushes back; he becomes a doctor in the end, but crucially _because he chose it_, not because the path was laid. Naoto Shirogane, born a woman with a passion for a male-dominated field, disguises herself to be taken seriously — told the path was closed, and walking it anyway. Chihaya Mifune, a gifted fortune-teller, is effectively held hostage by an organization that exploits her talent, and her struggle is to wrest her own gift back from the people using it to cage her. The crucial nuance: escape is not always the resolution. The Wheel turns either way, and the Fortune character's victory is putting their own hand on it — choosing their path freely, even when the chosen path and the fated one turn out to be the same.

**Don't confuse with —** the **Hanged Man**. Fortune struggles *against* a path fate set them on — "born to be a doctor, but I want to dance." The Hanged Man is frozen in the *crater* of a path that already collapsed — "I tried to dance, broke my leg, and can't imagine being anything else now." Fighting a destiny vs. stuck in a lost one.

### XI. Strength

Strength shows a woman gently closing a lion's jaws — not brute force but inner courage, patience, and the soft mastery of raw instinct. Reversed, it is self-doubt, insecurity, and a loss of nerve. The Strength archetype is about quiet, patient, behind-the-scenes fortitude rather than overt power — the resilience of the one who holds others up and is still searching for their own footing.

At their best, Strength characters are caring, resilient, encouraging, and quietly tough; at their worst they are aimless, uncertain of their own worth, and unsure they amount to anything on their own. The arc is finding one's own footing through the act of supporting others. Yuko Nishiwaki, a team manager who supports athletes rather than competing alongside them, discovers her purpose in helping others grow and resolves to build a life around it. Kou and Daisuke, two athletes, work through unrequited love and, for one of them, the harder question of where he belongs in a family that isn't biologically his. Play Strength as someone whose toughness is gentle, whose doubt is private, and whose growth is realizing that holding others up is a strength of its own, not a substitute for having a self.

### XII. The Hanged Man

The Hanged Man hangs serenely upside-down from a living tree, suspended by his own choice — surrender, suspension, sacrifice, and the world seen from a wholly different angle. Reversed, it is needless martyrdom, stalling, and stagnation: resistance to a change that has to come. The Hanged Man archetype is the person frozen in place, stuck or haunted by something they cannot put down, caught in a limbo that no amount of force will break.

At their best, Hanged Man characters are reflective, resilient, and capable of a hard-won new way of seeing; at their worst they are paralyzed — held fast by grief, loss, or a past they refuse to release. The arc is always about being stuck and learning to move past it through acceptance rather than force. Maiko Oohashi, a lonely child, is caught in the limbo of her parents' divorce and learns to cope with a family that has come apart. Naoki Konishi goes numb after his sister's murder, suffocating under the town's pity and gossip, until he can finally grieve honestly instead of performing the grief others expect. Munehisa Iwai, an ex-criminal suspended between his old life and a straight one he is trying to live for his foster son's sake, resolves at last to cut the ties holding him in place. Play the Hanged Man as someone who cannot be rushed out of their suspension — and whose growth is the slow work of acceptance that finally lets them down from the tree.

**Don't confuse with —** **Fortune**. The Hanged Man is frozen in the crater of a path that already collapsed; Fortune is still struggling against a path fate is *currently* setting them on. Stuck in a lost destiny vs. fighting a present one.

### XIII. Death

Death rides in armor as the old order falls before it — not literal dying but transformation, the ending that clears the ground for a beginning, the necessary close of one chapter so another can open. Reversed, it is resistance to that change: stagnation and the inability to let go. The Death archetype is the person standing in the shadow of mortality and loss, who must confront an ending before they can truly live again.

At their best, Death characters are wise about impermanence, compassionate, and able to help others let go; at their worst they are mired in survivor's guilt, fixated on the past, and numbed by grief into a kind of living death. The arc is confronting mortality in order to choose life on the far side of it. Hisano Kuroda, a widow consumed by guilt over her husband's death, finally forgives herself and resolves to keep living rather than wait to join him. Tae Takemi, a disgraced doctor obsessed with clearing her name over an old case, rediscovers in the process why she became a healer at all. Play Death as someone halted by a loss they have not finished grieving — and whose growth is the decision to keep living, changed but not buried.

### XIV. Temperance

Temperance shows an angel pouring liquid between two cups, one foot on land and one in water — balance, moderation, patience, and the blending of opposites into something whole. Reversed, it is excess and imbalance, two demands pulling a life apart. The Temperance archetype is the person living between two worlds or two roles, whose task is to blend them into something sustainable rather than be torn in half by them.

At their best, Temperance characters are patient, gentle, harmonizing, and devoted to bringing things or people together; at their worst they are spread too thin, exhausted by a divided life, and quietly buckling under demands that refuse to reconcile. The arc reconciles two incompatible worlds into a livable whole. Bebe, an exchange student devoted to a culture not his own, is torn between that love and the obligations waiting for him at home, and works to braid the two together rather than abandon either. Eri Minami, a young stepmother struggling to bond with a child who isn't hers by birth, slowly builds a real family out of an awkward one. Sadayo Kawakami burns herself out living a draining double life, and her growth is freeing herself enough to find a sustainable balance. Play Temperance as someone straddling two worlds at real cost — and whose growth is finding the proportion that lets them keep both.

**Don't confuse with —** the **Devil** and **Justice**. Temperance is pulled apart by two genuine goods it refuses to give up; the Devil clings to one chosen obsession and hides it; Justice holds a single absolute standard against a messy world. Two goods tearing them in half vs. one chain they won't drop vs. one principle they won't bend.

### XV. The Devil

The Devil shows two figures chained beneath a leering idol — but the chains hang loose enough to lift off at any time: bondage, obsession, and the shadow attachments we _choose_ to stay inside. Reversed, it is the moment of breaking free and reclaiming control. The Devil archetype is the most consistent once you look past the surface: outwardly charming, indulgent, worldly, and morally grey — but that presentation is a disguise, and underneath it sits an unhealthy obsession the person has chained themselves to. The charm is precisely how they avoid looking at it.

At their best, Devil characters are magnetic, candid about human weakness, and capable of slipping the chain to reclaim themselves; at their worst they are consumed — evasive, self-destructive, and willing to let the obsession run their lives while they pretend it's only appetite. The obsession is not always a base vice. President Tanaka is the simplest case, a man chained to money and the worship of it. But Sayoko Uehara is chained to her work caring for dying patients, and Ichiko Ohya to clearing a vanished colleague's name — virtues, devotion and loyalty, twisted into something that consumes the person holding them. That is the Devil's real range: the same chain forged in different metal, sometimes from greed and sometimes from a good impulse turned cannibalistic. Play the Devil as someone who hides an obsession behind charm — and whose growth, when it comes, is the recognition that the chain was never locked.

**Don't confuse with —** "Obsession" is the most over-claimed keyword in the deck, and the Devil is where it wrongly lands. The disqualifier is not "is the obsession noble?" — the Devil's chain can be a virtue turned cannibalistic — but "do they see it clearly, and do they value something the Devil would evade?" A character who genuinely *values* truth and reaches for a verdict is **Justice**, not the Devil. One torn between two real goods is **Temperance**. One hiding behind a comforting front from a fear they can't face is the **Moon** — a mask over fear, where the Devil is a chain they could lift any time and won't. The Devil is specifically the person who chose the chain and uses charm or a noble reason to avoid admitting it.

### XVI. The Tower

The Tower is struck by lightning, its crown blown off and figures falling from the heights — sudden upheaval, the violent collapse of false structures, and the painful release of pressure built up too long. Reversed, it is disaster narrowly avoided, or a reckoning merely delayed. The Tower archetype is the person living inside a false edifice — a role, an image, an unbearable expectation — that is about to come down, and whose collapse, however brutal, is what finally sets them free.

At their best, Tower characters are capable of profound, freeing breakthroughs and a hard-won humility; at their worst they are rigid, prideful, bottled-up, and one shock away from shattering. The arc is the collapse of a false structure to free the person trapped beneath it. Mutatsu is a drunk who has run from the family he lost, and his breakthrough is admitting he drinks to bury the pain and resolving to face them. Shu Nakajima is a brilliant, brittle child bent nearly to breaking by his mother's relentless academic pressure, who learns he does not have to be perfect to be loved. Shinya Oda hides from a domineering parent behind a cocky front, and finds the courage to confront the situation at home. Play the Tower as someone whose life is built on something that must fall — and whose growth begins only after it does.

### XVII. The Star

The Star shows a figure kneeling at the water's edge, pouring from two pitchers — one onto the land, one into the pool — replenishing the world beneath a sky of guiding lights. Upright, it is hope, faith, renewal, and serenity after devastation; reversed, it is despair and the light gone dim. But notice what the figure is actually _doing_: she is the source, pouring herself out so that others may be filled. The Star archetype lives inside that image — its arc is not about finding hope but about the struggle of _being_ hope for everyone else. From the outside they look successful, luminous, aspirational; inside they are carrying burdens they were handed and are not allowed to set down.

At their best, Star characters are genuinely inspiring, dedicated, and a real source of light for the people around them; at their worst they are depleted, trapped, and slowly erased behind the symbol others need them to be. Mamoru Hayase is a star athlete who is also his family's breadwinner — the hope is a function, and he cannot stop running even if he wants to. Teddie is the cheerful mascot of his group who is also, secretly, the very kind of thing they fight, and must somehow be a beacon while doubting he has any right to exist. Hifumi Togo is a celebrated prodigy whose family stakes its worth on her winning and being beautiful, turning her into an image before she is ever allowed to be a person. Play the Star as someone conscripted into being everyone else's hope — and whose growth is being allowed to set the pitcher down, because no one can pour from an empty vessel forever.

### XVIII. The Moon

The Moon shows a path winding between two towers past a howling dog and wolf, a crayfish crawling from the water — illusion, anxiety, fear, and the half-seen shapes of the subconscious that distort the truth. Reversed, it is the release of fear and the moment confusion gives way to clarity. The Moon archetype is the person ruled by anxiety and self-deception, hiding behind comforting illusions and a constructed front because the real self underneath feels unbearable to look at.

At their best, Moon characters are capable of deep honesty once they finally face themselves, and surprisingly resilient afterward; at their worst they are anxious, self-loathing, deceptive, and clinging to a flattering lie. The arc is facing the illusion to find the real self beneath it. Nozomi Suemitsu hides crippling insecurity behind a grandiose persona and is nearly swept into a doomsday scam before he learns to accept his ordinary self. Ai Ebihara masks deep self-hatred and loneliness behind materialism and cruelty, and her arc strips that down to something honest. Yuuki Mishima loses himself in hero-worship and a craving for recognition before confronting the selfishness driving it. Play the Moon as someone whose front is built over a fear they can't face — and whose growth is the painful honesty of looking directly at it.

**Don't confuse with —** the **Devil**. Both wear a front, but the Moon's is a comforting *illusion* over a fear too painful to look at, while the Devil's is *charm* over an obsession they chose and could set down. A mask over fear vs. a chain they could lift any time.

### XIX. The Sun

The Sun represents the slow transition from night to day. Traditional tarot frames it as the card of daylight already arrived — warmth, joy, vitality, the simple radiance of being fully alive — but the Sun's deeper meaning is the _coming_ of that light, the long dawn that begins while it is still dark. Its themes are perseverance, conviction, and the quiet moral force of choosing to generate warmth in circumstances that have given you no reason to. The Sun does not promise that things will be alright; it promises that something good can be made anyway.

Sun characters begin in genuine darkness — terminal illness, public disgrace, or a life that has not gone the way they hoped — and their arc is not about escaping these circumstances; some never do. Instead they refuse to let the darkness be the whole story. Akinari Kamiki, dying young of an incurable illness, spends his final months writing a children's story about meaning and connection, and dies having found peace and left something warm behind. Toranosuke Yoshida, a disgraced former politician ruined years ago by a charge that was really a rival's frame-job, keeps giving street speeches for a better society despite years of public humiliation, slowly earning his conviction back. At their worst, Sun characters are beaten down, bitter, and tempted to let despair define them; at their best they become a source of warmth for others precisely because they know the dark. Play the Sun as someone with every reason to give up who chooses to generate light anyway — and whose growth is making that warmth real for everyone who gets to stand near it, whether or not their own circumstances ever change.

### Other Arcana

Knowledgeable readers will note that The Fool, Judgement, and The World have been omitted. The Fool represents innocence, divine inspiration, madness, freedom, spontaneity, inexperience, chaos, and creativity. It should embody the player characters themselves as they go on their journey; those descriptors should sound familiar as a DM!

Judgement represents awakening, reckoning, and the moment when one finally hears a call that demands a response. It's more of a narrative moment for the player characters than a character in and of itself. Instead of creating an NPC representing Judgement, try to create a moment of reckoning for your players. Think of a confrontation that forces the party to face the consequences of past choices, or a revelation that demands they decide who they truly are. The strongest Judgement moments arrive when the players cannot hide from themselves any longer.

The World is the final Arcana, which represents the world's totality and the completion of the Fool's journey. Take care if you decide to create an NPC that embodies The World. These are complete characters that literally have no room for further growth. It isn't that they're perfect or god-like; it's that they've faced everything the world can throw at them and arrived at a final state of being. A character embodying The World is nearly impossible to write because you, the DM, are still on your own journey.
