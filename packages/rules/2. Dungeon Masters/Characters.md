### Arcana

When you create a Collaborator, it's helpful to assign them an Arcana. These don't necessarily have any mechanics attached to them; they're a helpful writing tool. The Collaborator's Arcana provides structure around their character arc and personality. Usually it's best to only create one Collaborator per Arcana, but don't let it stop you if you have a brilliant idea.

You also shouldn't feel pressure to match an Arcana with an Archetype lineage. A Magician character doesn't have to provide the Mage lineage, but it could. Feel free to experiment and think outside the box.

The characteristics and story arc that an Arcana provides are guidelines, but you should feel empowered to subvert them. The Hanged Man could represent an older man trying to escape his ties to organized crime or it could represent a little girl dealing with the divorce of her parents. Both characters are "stuck", but in different ways.

#### I. The Magician

The Magician represents the channeling of will into action. Where raw potential exists, the Magician is the figure who shapes it, taking the elements of the world and bending them toward a chosen end. The card carries themes of initiative, confidence, ambition, and self-expression, as well as the energy of youth taking its first deliberate steps into adulthood. The Magician is the spark that ignites a fire, the assertion that one's desires matter and are worth pursuing openly.

Magician characters are typically extroverted, energetic, and unshakably confident, often serving as a loud and loyal companion who throws themselves headlong into whatever excites them. They tend to wear their hearts on their sleeves, expressing affection, frustration, and conviction without much filter, which makes them easy to read but also prone to acting before thinking. Their arcs frequently involve learning to refine their drive into something purposeful, finding a cause worthy of their intensity, or reconciling their bravado with the more vulnerable feelings beneath it. At their worst, they are reckless, impulsive, and prone to overcompensation; at their best, they are catalysts who inspire others to act.

#### II. The High Priestess

The High Priestess represents intuition, hidden knowledge, and the inner world. She sits between the visible and the invisible, the conscious and the unconscious, possessing a wisdom that is felt rather than spoken. Her domain is mystery, contemplation, and the patient observation of truths others overlook, often carrying themes of femininity, secrets, and the sacred kept private. She is not passive so much as still, holding deep currents beneath a calm surface.

High Priestess characters tend to be poised, perceptive, and emotionally reserved, often appearing serene or even cold until trust is earned. They frequently carry burdens of duty or expectation, whether from family, faith, or station, and their arcs often involve learning to express their own desires rather than suppressing them in service of others. Beneath their composure lies considerable emotional depth and sometimes unexpected playfulness or yearning. At their worst, they are repressed, isolated, and unable to ask for what they need; at their best, they are quietly steadfast figures whose insight cuts to the heart of things.

#### III. The Empress

The Empress represents nurturing, abundance, and creative fertility. She is the archetype of motherhood in its broadest sense, encompassing not just literal parenthood but the act of caring for, growing, and sustaining others. Her themes include compassion, beauty, sensuality, and the cultivation of life, but also the shadow of overbearing care, smothering protection, and the loss of self in service to others. The Empress creates and tends, and her power lies in her capacity to provide.

Empress characters are typically warm, capable, and maternal in their bearing, often older than the player characters or positioned in a caretaking role, whether literal or figurative. They are frequently composed and competent on the surface but carry private griefs, regrets about past relationships, or anxieties about their ability to truly care for those they love. Their arcs often involve confronting the cost of always being the one who gives, learning to receive support, or reconciling with a child, parent, or partner from whom they have been estranged. At their worst, they are controlling, self-sacrificing to the point of martyrdom, or unable to let those they love grow independent; at their best, they are sources of profound stability and grace.

#### IV. The Emperor

The Emperor represents authority, structure, and the imposition of order on chaos. Where the Empress nurtures, the Emperor governs, establishing the rules, hierarchies, and frameworks within which others operate. His themes are leadership, discipline, tradition, and the legitimate use of power, but also rigidity, tyranny, and the cost of valuing systems over people. He is the father archetype in its commanding aspect, the figure who sets expectations and enforces consequences.

Emperor characters are typically confident, organized, and naturally inclined toward leadership, often holding positions of responsibility well beyond what their age would suggest. They tend to be principled and decisive, sometimes to a fault, and they often struggle with the loneliness of command or the rigidity of the rules they have imposed on themselves. Their arcs frequently involve learning that strength includes vulnerability, that rules must sometimes bend, or that the people they lead are not merely subordinates but peers. At their worst, they are domineering, inflexible, and unable to admit fault; at their best, they are dependable pillars whose conviction gives others something to rally around.

#### V. The Hierophant

The Hierophant represents tradition, institutions, and the transmission of received wisdom. Where the Magician channels personal will, the Hierophant channels the accumulated knowledge of a community, family, or faith. His themes include mentorship, ritual, conformity, and the comfort and constraint of belonging to something larger than oneself. He is the keeper of the old ways, and his presence asks whether one should uphold them, reform them, or break from them entirely.

Hierophant characters are often older figures who occupy roles of teacher, priest, craftsman, or patriarch, having dedicated themselves to preserving and passing on some tradition. They tend to be patient, principled, and grounded in their convictions, but their arcs frequently involve tension between the wisdom they have inherited and the changing world around them, including their own children or students who challenge their assumptions. They may struggle with regret over how they raised someone, with the obsolescence of their craft, or with reconciling old beliefs to new realities. At their worst, they are dogmatic and dismissive of younger generations; at their best, they are anchors who pass down meaningful inheritance while remaining open to growth.

#### VI. The Lovers

The Lovers represent connection, choice, and the union of opposites. Despite the name, the card is less about romance than about the meaningful choices we make in relationship to others, the moments when we decide who and what to commit ourselves to. Its themes include intimacy, harmony, values, and the way love (in all its forms) forces us to define ourselves. The Lovers ask what we are willing to choose and what we are willing to leave behind.

Lovers characters are shaped by the people and bonds that define them, often to the point where they struggle to find themselves outside those relationships. Their surface lives often look enviable — they tend to be warm, well-liked, and adept at the give and take of intimacy — but underneath sits a recurring question of who they would be if the relationships fell away. Their arcs almost always involve learning to define themselves on their own terms, to choose their path rather than accepting one chosen for them, and to love others without losing themselves. At their worst, they are dependent, indecisive, and shaped entirely by others' expectations; at their best, they are deeply loyal figures whose love is given freely and consciously rather than out of obligation.

#### VII. The Chariot

The Chariot represents drive, determination, and the will to victory. It is the card of forward motion, of harnessing opposing forces and pointing them toward a single goal. Its themes are ambition, courage, competition, and the discipline required to achieve what one sets out to do. The Chariot does not contemplate; it advances, and its strength comes from the refusal to be stopped.

Chariot characters are defined by a singular drive that organizes their entire life around it. They tend to be straightforward, honest, and easy to like, but they can be naive about anything outside their narrow focus and may struggle when their chosen path is threatened. Their arcs frequently involve setbacks that force them to question why they pursue their goal, to find meaning beyond winning, or to grow into a more complete person rather than a one-dimensional competitor. At their worst, they are tunnel-visioned, brash, and unable to handle failure; at their best, they are inspiring figures whose determination becomes a model for others.

#### VIII. Justice

Justice represents fairness, accountability, and the weighing of consequences. It is the card of moral clarity, of cause and effect, and of the demand that wrongs be acknowledged and addressed. Its themes include law, judgment, integrity, and the difficulty of acting rightly when the right action is costly. Justice does not ask what is convenient or popular; it asks what is true and what is owed.

Justice characters are often principled, serious, and uncompromising, frequently occupying roles connected to law, ethics, or institutional accountability. They tend to hold themselves to high standards and judge others by them as well, which can make them appear cold or self-righteous. Their arcs often involve confronting the gap between abstract principle and lived reality, learning that justice without compassion becomes cruelty, or reckoning with their own complicity in something they had condemned in others. At their worst, they are merciless, rigid, and quick to condemn; at their best, they are moral compasses whose integrity is genuinely earned through their willingness to apply their standards to themselves.

#### IX. The Hermit

The Hermit represents introspection, solitude, and the search for inner truth. He is the figure who withdraws from the world in order to understand it, carrying a lantern through the darkness of his own mind. His themes are wisdom, isolation, study, and the painful but necessary process of self-examination. The Hermit's path is lonely, but it leads to insights that cannot be gained any other way.

Hermit characters are typically introverted, intellectual, and socially awkward, often defined by an obsessive interest in some subject that others find strange or off-putting. They have frequently chosen isolation, whether out of shyness, past hurt, or genuine preference, and they tend to be more comfortable with books, flasks, or ideas than with people. Their arcs almost always involve gradually opening up, learning that their inner world is enriched rather than diminished by connection, and finding people who appreciate them as they are. At their worst, they are bitter, paranoid, and contemptuous of those they perceive as shallow; at their best, they are perceptive companions whose hard-won self-knowledge gives them a depth others lack.

#### X. Fortune

Fortune represents change, cycles, and the unpredictable turns of fate. It is the card of luck both good and bad, of circumstances beyond our control, and of the question of whether we shape our lives or merely react to what happens to us. Its themes include opportunity, reversal, destiny, and the tension between fatalism and agency. Fortune reminds us that nothing stays the same, for better or worse.

Fortune characters are often defined by a sense that their lives have been shaped by forces beyond their control, whether literal chance, family circumstance, or what they perceive as destiny. Their arcs frequently involve struggling against the cards fate has dealt them and reconciling their own desires with those cards. At their worst, they are passive, superstitious, or use "fate" to avoid responsibility; at their best, they are resilient figures who meet whatever comes with grace.

#### XI. Strength

Strength represents inner fortitude, gentle mastery, and the courage to face what frightens us. The classical image is of a small figure calming a lion through compassion rather than force, and the card's themes follow accordingly: patience, self-control, kindness as power, and the slow work of taming one's own fears and impulses. Strength is not the strength of domination but the strength of endurance and quiet conviction.

Strength characters are often outwardly unimposing, sometimes shy or anxious, but they reveal remarkable resilience when those they care about are threatened. They tend to have suffered something significant, whether illness, loss, bullying, or trauma, and their gentleness is hard-won rather than naive. Their arcs typically involve learning that their kindness is a strength rather than a weakness, finding the courage to assert themselves, or confronting the fear that has shaped their lives. At their worst, they are timid, self-effacing, and prone to letting others walk over them; at their best, they are deeply courageous figures whose softness coexists with an unbreakable core.

#### XII. The Hanged Man

The Hanged Man represents suspension, sacrifice, and the radical shift in perspective that comes from being unable to move. He hangs upside down, seeing the world inverted, surrendering action in exchange for insight. His themes are stagnation, surrender, hidden meaning, and the painful process of waiting through a transition that cannot be rushed. The Hanged Man's wisdom comes from the willingness to let go of control.

Hanged Man characters are often haunted figures, stuck in some way that others can see but they cannot escape, whether by guilt, obsession, trauma, or an inability to move past a defining event. They may seem cynical, detached, or self-destructive, but beneath their surface is usually a person searching for meaning in something that hurt them or that they did. Their arcs almost always involve a long, slow process of letting go, of either accepting what cannot be changed or finally taking action they have postponed for too long. At their worst, they are paralyzed, self-pitying, and unable to release the past; at their best, they are figures of hard-earned wisdom whose suffering has given them an unusual perspective.

#### XIII. Death

Death represents transformation, endings, and the necessary letting go that makes new life possible. Despite its grim imagery, the card is rarely about literal death and is more often about the closing of one chapter so another can begin. Its themes are mortality, change, renewal, and the grief that accompanies even welcome transitions. Death asks what we are willing to release in order to become who we are meant to be.

Death characters are typically marked by loss, often having lived close to mortality through profession, illness, or bereavement, and they carry a quiet familiarity with grief that sets them apart from others. They may seem morbid, melancholy, or unsettlingly calm, but their relationship with endings often gives them a clarity about what matters. Their arcs frequently involve helping others through transitions, processing their own unfinished grief, or finding a way to honor what has been lost without being defined by it. At their worst, they are nihilistic, withdrawn, or so accustomed to loss that they cannot form new attachments; at their best, they are profoundly compassionate figures who help others face what frightens them most.

#### XIV. Temperance

Temperance represents balance, integration, and the patient blending of opposites into something new. It is the card of moderation, healing, and the careful work of harmonizing contradictory forces within oneself or one's life. Its themes include synthesis, recovery, cultural exchange, and the recognition that wisdom often lies in the middle path rather than the extremes. Temperance is not compromise so much as alchemy.

Temperance characters often occupy positions between worlds, whether through mixed heritage, international experience, or a role that requires mediating between groups. They tend to be calm, thoughtful, and skilled at finding common ground, but their composure may hide their own struggles with feeling that they fully belong nowhere. Their arcs frequently involve embracing the parts of themselves they have suppressed to fit in, helping others reconcile differences, or finding peace with their dual nature. At their worst, they are conflict-averse to a fault, repressing their own needs in service of harmony; at their best, they are bridges between people who would otherwise never understand one another.

#### XV. The Devil

The Devil represents bondage, obsession, and the chains we forge for ourselves. Its themes include addiction, materialism, lust, manipulation, and the way that desire, taken to its extreme, becomes its own prison. The Devil is not evil so much as enslaving, representing the appetites and attachments that promise satisfaction but deliver dependency. The card also points to the truth that the chains are often looser than they appear, if one is willing to see them.

Devil characters are typically charming, indulgent, and morally compromised, often pursuing pleasure, success, or revenge with an intensity that has consumed other parts of their lives. They may be gamblers, hedonists, artists, or schemers, and they tend to be more self-aware about their flaws than they let on, hiding shame beneath bravado. Their arcs frequently involve confronting the cost of their obsessions, recognizing the people they have hurt, and finding the courage to choose differently even when their desires pull them back. At their worst, they are manipulative, self-destructive, and willing to drag others down with them; at their best, they are figures of hard-won honesty whose recovery is genuinely earned.

#### XVI. The Tower

The Tower represents sudden upheaval, the collapse of false structures, and the painful revelation of truths long denied. Its imagery is violent: a tower struck by lightning, figures falling from its heights. Its themes include catastrophe, awakening, the destruction of illusions, and the strange liberation that comes when what we built on lies finally crumbles. The Tower hurts, but it clears the ground for something more honest to be built.

Tower characters are often defined by a single shattering event, whether a betrayal, a scandal, a loss, or a revelation that destroyed the life they thought they had. They may appear angry, broken, or strangely free, and they typically possess a clarity about how fragile ordinary life is that others find unsettling. Their arcs frequently involve choosing what to build from the ruins, refusing to rebuild the same illusions, and reckoning with the question of whether the collapse was ultimately a curse or a release. At their worst, they are destructive, nihilistic, or determined to tear down others' illusions as theirs were torn down; at their best, they are figures of brutal honesty whose suffering has burned away pretense.

#### XVII. The Star

The Star represents hope, healing, and the quiet renewal that follows catastrophe. Where the Tower destroys, the Star offers gentle restoration, the sense that even after everything has been lost, something tender and luminous remains. Its themes are inspiration, faith, guidance, and the long process of recovering from wounds that once seemed mortal. The Star does not promise easy answers, but it promises that the night is not endless.

Star characters are often figures of inspiration to others, whether artists, athletes, performers, or public figures whose presence offers some form of hope. They have typically suffered greatly and emerged with a fragile but genuine optimism, and their gift is the ability to help others believe in possibility again. Their arcs frequently involve reconciling the public role they play with their private struggles, learning to receive the hope they so freely give, or finding the strength to continue when their own light feels dim. At their worst, they are exhausted by the expectations of others, performing optimism they no longer feel; at their best, they are genuine sources of light whose hope is real precisely because it has been tested.

#### XVIII. The Moon

The Moon represents illusion, the unconscious, and the disorienting territory where dreams, fears, and half-truths blur together. Its themes include deception, intuition, anxiety, and the difficulty of trusting what we perceive when the path is lit only by reflected light. The Moon is not necessarily malevolent, but it is treacherous, and crossing its landscape requires accepting that some things cannot be known clearly, only navigated by feel.

Moon characters are often unsettling figures, defined by ambiguity, secrets, or a quality that makes others uncertain whether to trust them. They may be liars, fantasists, manipulators, or simply people so haunted by their own delusions that they no longer recognize what is real. Their arcs frequently involve confronting the truth they have been hiding from, whether from others or from themselves, and choosing whether to step into honest light or remain in comforting shadow. At their worst, they are deceptive, paranoid, and destructive to those who try to help them; at their best, they are figures of hard-won clarity who have crossed their own darkness and emerged able to help others through theirs.

#### XIX. The Sun

The Sun represents the slow transition from night to day. Traditional tarot frames it as the card of daylight already arrived — warmth, joy, vitality, the simple radiance of being fully alive — but the Sun's deeper meaning is the _coming_ of that light, the long dawn that begins while it is still dark. Its themes are perseverance, conviction, and the quiet moral force of choosing to generate warmth in circumstances that have given you no reason to. The Sun does not promise that things will be alright. It promises that something good can be made anyway.

Sun characters begin in genuine darkness — illness, disgrace, irreversible loss, or the slow grind of a life that has not gone the way they hoped. Their arc is not about overcoming these circumstances; many of them never do. Instead, they refuse to let the darkness be the whole story. They commit to something — a creative project, a principle, a person, a craft — and they pursue it not out of optimism but out of the conviction that it is worth pursuing regardless of the outcome. As they persist, they begin to illuminate the people around them without trying to, and the warmth they generate becomes real even when their own circumstances do not change. At their worst, they are bitter, performatively cheerful, or so committed to their stance that they cannot accept help; at their best, they are figures whose perseverance is genuinely transformative — not for themselves, necessarily, but for everyone who got to stand near their light.

#### Other Arcana

Knowledgeable readers will note that The Fool, Judgement, and The World have been omitted. The Fool represents innocence, divine inspiration, madness, freedom, spontaneity, inexperience, chaos, and creativity. It should embody the player characters themselves as they go on their journey; those descriptors should sound familiar as a DM!

Judgement represents awakening, reckoning, and the moment when one finally hears a call that demands a response. It's more of a narrative moment for the player characters than a character in and of itself. Instead of creating an NPC representing Judgement, try to create a moment of reckoning for your players. Think of a confrontation that forces the party to face the consequences of past choices, or a revelation that demands they decide who they truly are. The strongest Judgement moments arrive when the players cannot hide from themselves any longer.

The World is the final Arcana, which represents the world's totality and the completion of the Fool's journey. Take care if you decide to create an NPC that embodies The World. These are complete characters that literally have no room for further growth. It isn't that they're perfect or god-like; it's that they've faced everything the world can throw at them and arrived at a final state of being. A character embodying The World is nearly impossible to write because you, the DM, are still on your own journey.
