Your character's **Identity** is who they are at the moment play begins — their habits, their hopes, their fears, and the things they hide. Where your Virtues describe what your character is good at, your Origin Archetype describes what they have trained to do, and your Knives and Chains describe what they care about and what is holding them back, Identity describes *how* those things are expressed — who, exactly, you add up to as a person.

Identity is composed of five kinds of traits, referred to collectively as **Identity Traits**: Personality Traits, Hopes, Dreams, Fears, and Secrets. Build your Identity Traits last in the character creation process, after your Virtues, Origin, Knives, and Chains are settled — those earlier choices give you the raw material from which Identity Traits emerge naturally.

Share your Identity Traits with your DM before play begins. Like Knives and Chains, they give the DM material to make your character's story matter at the table.

## Personality Traits

A Personality Trait is a small, specific habit or quirk that makes your character recognizable at the table. Maybe they talk with a lisp. Maybe they only wear the most expensive clothing they can afford. Maybe they are the party's class clown, or maybe they are painfully shy in any group larger than two. The strongest Personality Traits are things another player at the table could mimic after one session.

Choose **two to four** Personality Traits. Avoid traits that are simply restatements of your Virtues; "articulate" and "wise" describe a Rank, not a person. Reach for texture instead: *how* does your Expression show up at the table? Do they crack jokes when frightened or do they go silent and still? If your character has high Wisdom, are they a know-it-all jerk about it? Or do they act as a genial mentor to the party?

## Hopes

A Hope is a short-term, realistic goal your character is actively working toward. Hopes are the engine of your character's near-term decisions, and they should be concrete enough that you and the DM can recognize when one is fulfilled.

Choose **one or two** Hopes. Good Hopes feel achievable within the next few levels of play: earning enough gold to rebuild your razed village, finding your missing sister, or getting accepted into the city's guild of mages. When a Hope is fulfilled, you can replace it with a new one.

## Dreams

A Dream is a long-term, larger-than-life goal your character cannot achieve alone — and may not achieve in their lifetime. Where Hopes drive your character's next session, Dreams drive their whole life. Examples include ending a century-long war between two kingdoms, finding a cure for a devastating plague, or [creating a world without lawyers](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=uG3uea-Hvy4).

Choose **one** Dream. A Dream may shift over the course of a campaign as your character grows, and that shift itself can be a powerful character moment. A Dream rarely resolves cleanly the way a Hope does; instead, it shapes what your character notices, what they sacrifice for, and what they refuse to compromise on. A character who dreams of curing the plague might perk up at the rumor of a miraculous cure-all discovered a town over.

## Fears

A Fear is something that paralyzes your character *right now* but which they can plausibly overcome through the events of the campaign. Every Fear emerges from a specific trauma in your character's past. "Afraid of realtors" is a joke Personality Trait, but "afraid of realtors because you can't bear to move out of the house your husband died in" is a Fear. The fear itself (whether concrete or abstract) is important, but what matters more is the wound underneath it. Without that wound, a fear is just a phobia, and a phobia is a Personality Trait.

Choose **one or two** Fears. Fears often tie directly to your Knives or Chains. A Chain born from a past failure naturally produces a Fear of failing again; a Knife you cherish naturally produces a Fear of losing it. Confronting and overcoming a Fear is generally a smaller, more iterative process than breaking a Chain.

## Secrets

A Secret is something only your character (and perhaps a very small circle of others) knows, and which would be devastating if revealed. A Secret does not have to be about your character — it might be that you accidentally killed your brother, or it might be that you know the King is a Lich. If your Secret is external (like the Lich example), work with the DM to make sure it fits into the world they've created. 

Choose **one or two** Secrets. Share each Secret with your DM in private; together you can decide which other characters, if any, share it. A Secret is a slow-burning fuse the DM can light at any time, and the threat of exposure is often as dramatically useful as the exposure itself. Consider how your character would react if each Secret were exposed, or even threatened with exposure; the DM will be thinking about it too.
