Every character has an Ancestry, a Background, and a Backstory. Ancestry and Background are defined by your campaign setting rather than this rulebook — think of these sections as slots waiting to be filled by the world your DM has created. The Backstory rules listed here are setting-agnostic.

## Ancestry

Ancestry is a broad term that means different things in different settings. In a high fantasy world, your Ancestry might determine your Species; whether you're an elf, a dwarf, or a dragonborn, each with distinct abilities and traits. In a more grounded setting, everyone might be human, but belong to different cultures or bloodlines that shape who they are. Consult your campaign setting for available Ancestries and what they provide.

## Background

Your Background represents what your character did before the adventure began. Were you a soldier, a scholar, a street thief, or a disgraced noble? Your Background grounds your character in the world and typically provides equipment, Talents, or Attribute/Virtue bonuses that reflect your prior experience. Consult your campaign setting for available Backgrounds and what they provide.

## Backstory

Your Backstory is the story of your life before the adventure begins and the shape of who your character is trying to become. A good Backstory isn’t just your character’s history (although that is important). It should explain how your character came to be this way and what continues to affect them.

Share your Backstory with your DM before play begins. They need this material to make your character's story matter at the table.

### Knives

A Knife is a person, place, or thing your character cares about deeply enough that losing it would hurt. Knives create dramatic stakes — they give the world something to threaten and give you something to fight for.

When you write your Backstory, aim for around seven Knives. Fewer than four and the world has little to threaten. More than twelve and you'll overwhelm your DM. Good Knives are specific: not "my family" but "my younger sister Mira, who I promised I'd come back to." The more concrete a Knife, the more the DM can do with it.

Examples of Knives:

- A person you love or owe a debt to
- A place that feels like home
- A belief or ideal you'd sacrifice for
- A reputation you've spent years building
- An enemy you haven't finished with yet

Each Knife is the opportunity to gain a [[1.6 Level Advancement|Victory]]. If the DM decides to stab you with your “Mira” Knife by having her kidnapped, finding her and bringing the kidnapper to justice (and earning a Victory in the process) provides both a character moment and a mechanical advantage.

### Chains

A Chain is something inside you holding you back from who you're meant to be. Where Knives are external, Chains are internal — the fears, wounds, and lies you've internalized about yourself.

A Chain might be a crippling self-doubt born from a past failure. It might be a vow of vengeance that's slowly consuming you. It might be a belief that you don't deserve to be loved, or that your power makes you dangerous to everyone around you. Whatever form it takes, a Chain is the gap between who you are and who you could be.

You can start with one significant Chain or several smaller ones. Breaking a Chain is a major character moment — it doesn't happen through grinding or accumulation, it happens when you've genuinely confronted and overcome whatever was holding you back. When your last Chain breaks, you've reached the fullest expression of who your character is and your Paragon Archetype becomes available.

Chains can also be gained through play. A knife wound deep enough can become a Chain — a trauma, a betrayal, or a loss that leaves a lasting mark on who your character is. If something happens in the story that fundamentally changes your character's relationship with themselves, talk to your DM about whether a new Chain has formed.
