A character will sometimes need to take a chance to overcome a moment of uncertainty. Leaping over a collapsing floor calls for Agility. Brewing a delicate potion calls for Focus. Reading a stranger's intentions calls for Empathy. In these moments, the DM will call for a **Talent Test**.

A Talent Test pairs one of your Attributes or Virtues with a Talent — a specific area of training your character has developed.

## Talents

Your active Archetype grants you a set of Talents. Your Background grants more, reflecting what your character did before the adventure began.

You can learn a new Talent by spending five downtime activity slots practicing it. You must reasonably be able to learn it in your current circumstances; you can't pick up Alchemy stranded on a rowboat in the middle of the ocean. Even if you had your "Alchemy for Dummies" book with you, you'd still need a bunch of alchemy tools to practice. The DM may also require you to apprentice under an NPC, find a specific tome, or acquire specialized equipment.

> [!info] D&D Skills
> If you're coming from D&D, Talents are this system's version of skills (Stealth, Persuasion, etc.). The word 'Skill' in this system always refers to a combat ability cast in battle, like Agi or Cleave.

## Making a Talent Test

The DM will call for a Test using a specific Attribute or Virtue and set a target, called a **DC** (short for "Difficulty Class"). The shorthand is **DC X (Stat) Test** — for example, *DC 15 Wisdom Test*.

Roll a d20 and add either the Attribute's score or the Virtue's rank. If you have a Talent that reasonably applies, suggest it to the DM. If they accept, add **+3**. Only one Talent's bonus applies to any given Test, even if multiple could plausibly fit.

There is no fixed pairing between Talents and stats. The same Talent can support different stats depending on how you describe your approach, and the DM may call for different stats depending on what you describe. Intimidating with a sharp stare is Expression; intimidating by smashing a brick wall with your fist is Strength.

> **DM:** Your party turns the corner. Suddenly, you all hear a quiet *click*. The floor begins to retract, revealing a lava pit!
>
> **Rogue:** I leap to safety!
>
> **DM:** Simple enough, give me a DC 12 Agility Test.
>
> **Rogue:** I have the Athletics Talent — sticking a landing on a moving floor counts, right?
>
> **DM:** Sure, +3.
>
> **Rogue:** Rolled a 7. Plus 3 Agility, plus 3 from Athletics. That's 13.
>
> **DM:** You spring back just as the floor falls away.
>
> **Barbarian:** I grab our Wizard and our Ranger under my arms and sprint backward.
>
> **DM:** Sounds like you're using Strength, but carrying two people is tough. Give me a DC 18 Strength Test.
>
> **Barbarian:** Can I use my Lift Talent to help carry them?
>
> **DM:** Definitely applies.
>
> **Barbarian:** Rolled a 12, plus 4 Strength, plus 3 from Lift. 19 total.
> 
> **DM:** Awesome, you scoop up the Wizard and Ranger and back-step to safety as fast as you can.

If the DM rejects your Talent suggestion, you can rethink your approach before rolling — a different angle might call for a different stat or a different Talent.

> [!info] The DM's Role
> Don't call for your own Talent Tests — that's the DM's job. The DM may not be asking because the task is trivially easy or flatly impossible.
>
> Instead of *"I make a Magic Test to dispel the barrier,"* try *"I attempt to dispel the barrier"* or *"DM, can I make a Magic Test to dispel the barrier?"*

Rolling a natural 1 or natural 20 has no special rule on a Talent Test; they do not automatically succeed or fail. A character with Rank 6 Empathy and a relevant Talent won't fail a DC 10 Test even on a natural 1 — their minimum total is 10. Conversely, a character asking the King to hand over his throne shouldn't succeed on a natural 20; the king takes it as a joke and warms to them instead.

> [!info] Failed Empathy Tests
> If a player asks the DM whether they believe a lying NPC and they fail the Test, some DMs will tell the player they believe the NPC. Instead, the DM should tell the player they can't get a read on the NPC. Allow the player to decide whether they believe the NPC or not.
>
> That said, encourage roleplay. If the *character* is gullible, maybe they *would* believe the Sketchy Merchant even if the *player* knows something is up. Conversely, a highly skeptical character might disbelieve an honest NPC if they fail the Test. The separation between what the player knows and what their character knows is vital to creating dramatic irony.

> [!info] Partial Failures
> The DM may allow partial successes or failures depending on the roll. If you fall *just* short, the DM may allow you to succeed with a complication. Perhaps you convince the guard in the moment, but they remain suspicious and begin investigating your party.

## Talent Gates

Some activities require a minimum Attribute score or Virtue rank. The DM might decide that infiltrating a high-end social club requires at least Rank 3 Expression to properly fit in. Lifting a ridiculously heavy boulder might require both the Lift Talent *and* 4+ Strength.

There's no hard rule on when to use a Gate instead of a Test. Generally, Gates represent sustained capability rather than a lucky moment. A character might say exactly the right thing on a good roll, but some situations require demonstrated, consistent competence that only a sufficient Attribute score or Virtue rank can provide.

> **Player:** I saw signs for a mage academy! Can I enroll to improve my spells?
>
> **DM:** What's your Wisdom?
>
> **Player:** Rank 1…
>
> **DM:** Sorry, the mage academy has high standards. You'd need at least Rank 3 to enroll. If you spent a few weeks studying in the library, you could raise your Wisdom that high.

It's up to the DM whether everyone in the party needs to clear a Gate or only one character. In our social club example:

> **Social Club Manager:** Welcome to the Dragon's Nest. How may I help you?
>
> **Suave Player:** Hello good sir, we'd like to enter your fine establishment.
>
> **DM:** What's your Expression?
>
> **Suave Player:** Rank 4.
>
> **DM:** Definitely high enough to enter. However…
>
> **Social Club Manager:** Well, you certainly clear our high bar. You would be a valuable member in our society. However, I can't say the same for your companions… is he drooling?
>
> **Suave Player:** Ah, worry not my friend. I vouch for them all.
>
> **DM:** Make an Expression Test. Your target is 18.
>
> **Suave Player:** I have Persuade — does that apply?
>
> **DM:** Sure does.
>
> **Suave Player:** Rolled a 13, plus 4 Expression, plus 3 from Persuade. 20 total.
>
> **Social Club Manager:** Hmm, very well. But if I hear any complaints from our patrons, I will need to ask you all to leave.

If the Suave Player had failed, perhaps the rest of the party would need to remain outside. If the party doesn't want to split up, they'll need to come up with a new plan.

## Combining Gates and Tests

Gates and Tests can work together. A mysterious tome written in a lost language might require Rank 3 Wisdom just to read. Once the Gate is cleared, finding a specific passage under time pressure might require a Focus Test. In this way, Gates and Tests create layered challenges with multiple possible outcomes.
