Time is one of your character's most important resources, and it cannot be recovered once spent. A campaign moves on whether you do or not, and when an event has a fixed deadline, your DM will tell you. These deadlines are real. If you know a cult will summon the Demon King by the next eclipse and they aren't stopped, the Demon King will rise and the campaign will continue from there.

You will usually have only one active deadline at a time. Other events may loom in the background, but those will surface when their time comes.

This chapter describes how time is tracked at three scales. **Daily activities** divide downtime into time slots. **Travel** is measured in days on the road. **Dungeon turns** measure exploration in ten-minute increments.

## Daily Activities

The time between adventures is yours to spend. How you use it shapes your character; your Virtues, your Collaborators, and the resources you bring into the next adventure are usually advanced through downtime.

### Time Slots

A standard day has two **time slots**: morning and evening. Each slot fits one substantial activity. A few activities take both slots, or several days.

You don't spend a slot to rest. Eating, sleeping, and recovery happen as a matter of course. The mechanical effects of resting are covered in [[2.5 Resting & Exhaustion]].

Adventuring fills slots quickly. Travel, dungeon delves, and high-stakes investigations can consume one or both slots, or several days at once. Your DM will tell you when a day's slots are spoken for.

> [!info] Setting the Slots
> Morning and evening are defaults. A campaign set in space, on another plane, or beneath the sea may have no meaningful day or night, and your DM is free to rename or restructure the slots. Whatever the labels, two activity slots per day is the standard.

### What You Can Do

Most downtime activities fall into three categories:

- **Virtue activities** are tied to a specific Virtue and grant a Spark in that Virtue when completed. Studying ancient tomes builds Wisdom; performing in a tavern builds Expression. See [[1.2 Attributes and Virtues#Ranking Up Virtues]].
- **Collaborator activities** deepen your bonds with key NPCs, unlocking new Archetypes, abilities, or story paths. See [[2.3 Social Interaction#Collaborators]].
- **Practical activities** include brewing potions, sharpening weapons, gathering rumors, or scouting an upcoming [[2.3 Social Interaction#Negotiation|Negotiation]] target.

Some activities are solo, letting party members pursue different goals on the same day. Others require the whole party. The activity entry will say which.

### Choosing What to Do

Your DM will provide a list of available activities, drawn from a standard set and supplemented with options specific to your location, the season, recent events, or the time of day. You may also propose your own. Most reasonable proposals will succeed, though some require certain tools or knowledge—you can't brew potions without an alchemy set and a recipe.

### Trade-offs

Two slots a day is not a lot. When a deadline looms, time spent training is time not spent traveling, and time spent fighting is time not spent preparing. A party with seven days to save the prince and a three-day journey ahead probably doesn't have room for a four-day round trip to the Capital for fancy new equipment.

## Traveling

How long a journey takes depends on distance and method. Walking is slow but available to anyone; horses are faster but need rest and feed; a hired ship cuts weeks off a coastal route but follows wind and weather; a train across the empire runs on its own schedule. The full travel rules are in [[2.4 Exploration]].

### What You Can Do on the Road

Whether you can spend time slots while traveling depends on how much attention the journey demands. Walking and riding usually consume both slots. A passenger on a train, an airship, or a chartered carriage often has both slots free. Crewing your own ship falls in the middle—you might find time for an evening's work, but the morning belongs to the rigging. Your DM will tell you which slots, if any, the journey leaves you.

### Complications

Roads are rarely empty. Storms, washed-out bridges, bandits, hungry beasts, and travelers in need are all part of moving through the world. The longer the journey and the wilder the route, the more likely you are to run into something. Plan your method and your route accordingly.

### Local Travel

Getting from one place to another inside a city or town does not consume a time slot. Walking across the capital to meet a contact, riding the underground to the docks, or knocking on a neighbor's door is part of whatever activity you are doing.

## Dungeon Turns

Inside a dungeon, time is measured in **dungeon turns**. Each turn represents about ten minutes of careful exploration—long enough to thoroughly search a passage, fiddle with a lock, or catch your breath.

### Taking Turns

Each dungeon turn, every player chooses one action. There is no fixed order, and players don't have to do the same thing—one character can search the library while another picks the lock on the chapel door and a third stands watch.

Most actions are scoped to a **Zone**, the same regions of space described in [[3.5 Zones & Movement]]. Searching, investigating, or disabling traps in a Zone applies to the whole Zone. For example, when you take the Find Secrets action, you discover all hidden features whose DC is at or below your roll; you don't check each one individually. A list of standard dungeon turn actions appears at the end of this chapter.

Moving at a normal pace between adjacent Zones does not consume a dungeon turn. Searching for traps along the way or moving stealthily does.

### Combat

A fight consumes the dungeon turn in which it happens, including time spent catching your breath and looting the bodies.

### Random Encounters

Dungeons are not still. As you explore, your DM will periodically check for an encounter. These encounters could be hostile monsters, but they don't have to be. You might run into a merchant who made the poor decision to set up shop in a forgotten crypt or hear the sounds of a wandering Displacer Beast as it searches for prey.

### The Length of a Day

A normal expedition runs about eight hours, or 48 dungeon turns. Beginning on the 49th turn, every additional half-hour (every 3 turns) imposes one level of **Exhaustion** on each character.

Exploring a dungeon consumes the rest of the day. The hours after a delve are spent traveling back to town, if one is close by, or setting up camp for the night. There is no leftover time slot for a downtime activity.

### Resting in a Dungeon

You cannot Partial Rest inside a dungeon. The only rest available between fights is a **Respite**—a 20-minute pause (two dungeon turns) that lets each character roll Hit Dice to recover HP. The full rest rules are in [[2.5 Resting & Exhaustion]]. Take care to manage your SP while dungeon diving; SP-restorative items are rare.

### Dungeon Actions

The following are the most common actions a character can take during a dungeon turn. This list isn't exhaustive — propose new actions to your DM as the situation calls for them.

#### Help

Choose another character in your Zone. They have Advantage on the next Talent Test they make this turn. You must be able to plausibly contribute to their task; you can't help disarm a trap from across the room.

#### Interact

You spend the turn handling an object or feature of the environment — reading a journal, clearing rubble, hauling open a stuck door, tinkering with a strange device. Your DM will tell you if the work calls for a Talent Test.

#### Keep Watch

You stand alert, eyes on the corridors and doors. Hostile creatures within your sight cannot Ambush the party (see [[3.2 Initiating Combat & Turn Order]]). If combat begins this turn, you may also take a single Standard Action before the first round begins.

#### Loot

You gather any valuables or gear in the Zone that have already been discovered or that sit in plain sight. Hidden caches require the Search action.

#### Move Quietly

**Party Wide**. You cross into an adjacent Zone at a careful, silent pace. Make an Agility Test. Until the start of the next combat encounter, you have Advantage on Ambush rolls.

#### Search

You comb a Zone for hidden features — secret doors, pressure plates, concealed compartments, a coin dropped behind a chest. Make a Focus Test. You discover everything in the Zone with a DC at or below your result.

#### Take Your Time

You spend two dungeon turns instead of one on another action, planning every step and re-checking your work. Treat the d20 as a 20 on the Talent Test for that action. If the Test would have had Advantage, add +5 to the result instead.

> [!info] Retrying a Failed Test
> Your DM may let you spend an extra dungeon turn retrying a recently failed Talent Test as a Take Your Time activity.
