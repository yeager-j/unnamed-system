Combat is only one of the encounters your party will face. Most of your time will be spent talking—to merchants and innkeepers, to allies and rivals, to nobles whose help you need and prisoners whose silence you'd prefer to break. The DM speaks for every NPC the party meets, and these encounters are resolved through a combination of roleplay and Virtue Tests. A clever line of argumentation or a perfectly chosen lie can change the course of a campaign as much as a Critical Hit.

## NPC Attitudes

Every NPC has an attitude toward the party that shapes how they respond to social interactions. There are six attitudes, listed from least to most receptive.

| Attitude   | Description                                                                                |
| ---------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Hostile    | Openly opposed to the heroes. Barely willing to listen.                                    |
| Suspicious | Doubts the heroes' motives but is willing to listen.                                       |
| Neutral    | Doesn't feel one way or the other.                                                         |
| Open       | Willing to listen, willing to help, as long as the heroes aren't asking too much.          |
| Friendly   | The heroes seem like the NPC's people. The NPC gives them the benefit of the doubt.        |
| Trusting   | The NPC has reason to take the heroes at their word and will help unless they screw it up. |

An NPC's attitude affects both the difficulty and the feasibility of [[2.1 Talent Tests#Making a Talent Test|Talent Tests]] directed at them. The DM sets DCs in light of the NPC's current attitude. Convincing a Friendly innkeeper to extend your tab might be a DC 10 Expression Test; convincing a Suspicious one might be DC 18; a Hostile one might refuse to entertain the request at all, no matter the result of a roll. Conversely, a Trusting NPC may grant a request without requiring a Test — they trust you, and that's enough.

Attitudes can shift in response to events at the table. Threatening a Friendly NPC will quickly drop them to Suspicious or Hostile. Saving a Suspicious NPC's life may push them to Open, or even Friendly. The DM tracks attitude shifts behind the scenes; the players will usually feel the change in how the NPC speaks to them rather than being told outright.

> [!info] Attitudes and Negotiation
> An NPC's attitude also determines the starting values of Interest and Patience in a Negotiation. See [[2.3 Social Interaction#Negotiation|Negotiation]], below.

## Roleplaying

Roleplaying means making decisions from your character's perspective rather than your own. Your character is a separate person, with their own values, fears, and blind spots. What's obvious to you as a player may be invisible to them, and what they care about may bore you to tears. Leaning into that gap is the bread and butter of roleplaying games.

### Making Choices In-Character

When deciding how your character would act in a specific situation, look at the pieces of them you've already built. Your [[1.2 Attributes and Virtues#Social Virtues|Virtue scores]] tell you what your character is naturally good and bad at. A Rank 1 Empathy character probably doesn't notice when someone is upset; they aren't being callous, they're being unobservant. Your [[1.3 Archetypes#Creating a New Character|Origin Archetype]] tells you the foundation of how your character moves through the world—a Mage Origin might see problems as puzzles to solve, while a Warrior Origin might see them as obstacles to overcome. Your [[1.4 Character Origins#Knives|Knives]] tell you what your character will fight for and where they're vulnerable; they should perk up at any mention of those things, for good or ill. Your [[1.4 Character Origins#Chains|Chains]] tell you what your character can't yet do—a character chained by self-doubt won't volunteer themselves for the dangerous mission, even if you the player know they're the best for it. Finally, your [[1.5 Character Identity|Identity Traits]] fill in the texture of how your character speaks and reacts in the moment.

### Acting Out Your Character

If you're new to roleplaying, or you just feel uncomfortable putting on a voice, you don't have to. Narrate in the third person instead. *"Joker leans against the wall and asks the bartender what he knows about the disappearances"* is just as valid as voicing the question directly. As you grow more comfortable, you can shift into first person and add voice when it feels natural. The goal is for the table to know what your character does and says, not for you to perform.

> [!info] Metagaming and Dramatic Irony
> Metagaming is when a player makes decisions based on information their character doesn't have. The classic example from Dungeons & Dragons is a Troll's weakness to fire. You the player might know that fire prevents their regeneration, but would your character?
> 
> Try to act on what your *character* knows, even when you the player know more. The gap between the two is where dramatic irony lives, and dramatic irony is one of the best gifts you can give the rest of the table. Watching the other players squirm because they can see the trap closing in is a feature, not a bug.
> 
> That said, metagaming isn't necessarily wrong. It depends on the table. A table doing a grueling dungeon crawl, where the objective is to complete the dungeon and not play out a story, might be fine with metagaming.

## Collaborators

A Collaborator is a recurring NPC the party comes to know personally and develops a relationship with over the course of the campaign. Collaborators are not party members (they may not even share the party's goals) but they are people whose lives become tangled with yours: a court wizard who tutors you between adventures, a thieves' guild contact who lets you in on the gossip, a barkeep who keeps a table open for you when you ride back into town.

Collaborators are mechanically important for two reasons.

First, DMs can use Collaborators to control access to **Archetype Lineages**. Some Collaborators are associated with a specific Lineage, and befriending them is what unlocks that Lineage for your character. Deepening the relationship through downtime spent together, sidequests on their behalf, or pivotal moments at their side unlocks the higher tiers within that Lineage. Befriending the kingdom's court wizard might unlock the Mage Lineage; advancing her personal story might unlock the Wizard tier above it.

Second, Collaborators are a powerful source of **Victories**. Helping a Collaborator through a personal crisis is a meaningful narrative win *and* is often rewarded with a Victory.

For these reasons, spending time with your Collaborators is one of the most valuable things you can do during downtime. It deepens existing relationships, opens new Archetype possibilities, and gives the DM hooks they can use to weave your character into the world.

## Negotiation

A Negotiation is a high-stakes conversation with an NPC who is open to making a deal but has a reason not to simply agree. Convincing the local lord to lend you a company of soldiers to march on the Demon King is a reasonable request. Demanding that he hand over his crown is not. Negotiation is not a process that changes an NPC’s character. Rather, the players are trying to make an NPC understand how behaving differently would be in character.

Unlike ordinary social interactions, a Negotiation has a specific structure. The NPC has two stats, Interest and Patience, that the players raise or lower through their arguments. The NPC also has two hidden traits, Motivations and Pitfalls, that determine which arguments land and which backfire. The Negotiation ends when one of those tracks reaches its limit, or when the players choose to walk away.

### Starting a Negotiation

Negotiations are initiated by the players. When an NPC asks something of the party, the players can simply refuse without entering a Negotiation. If the players want something from an NPC and the NPC isn’t willing to grant it freely, the players announce that they’d like to negotiate.

A Negotiation can also grow out of combat, but only if both sides are willing. An enemy who clearly has the upper hand will rarely agree to talk; an enemy on the verge of defeat usually will.

### Interest and Patience

Both Interest and Patience are tracked on a scale from **1 to 6**.

**Interest** measures how close the NPC is to agreeing to the players’ proposal. Interest rises and falls based on the arguments the players make. If Interest reaches **6**, the Negotiation ends in success — the NPC agrees and sweetens the deal of their own accord. If Interest drops to **1**, the Negotiation ends in failure — the NPC refuses, and the players have made an enemy.

**Patience** measures how much longer the NPC is willing to keep talking. Patience can only stay the same or decrease; it never rises during a Negotiation. If Patience drops to **1**, the NPC cuts the conversation short and makes a final offer based on their current Interest (see *Final Offers*, below).

The DM sets the starting values of Interest and Patience based on the NPC’s attitude toward the party.

| Attitude   | Interest | Patience |
| ---------- | -------- | -------- |
| Hostile    | 2        | 3        |
| Suspicious | 3        | 3        |
| Neutral    | 3        | 4        |
| Open       | 4        | 4        |
| Friendly   | 4        | 5        |
| Trusting   | 4        | 6        |

> [!info] Shared Language
> If at least one player shares a language other than the setting's standard language (in the Forgotten Realms, Common is a standard language) with the NPC, Patience starts **1 higher** than the table indicates. If three or more players share a language with the NPC, Patience starts **2 higher**. Patience can never start above 6.

### Motivations and Pitfalls

Every NPC enters a Negotiation with at least **two Motivations** and at least **one Pitfall**. These are not visible to the players at the start.

A **Motivation** is something the NPC genuinely cares about — a value, a desire, a fear of losing something. An argument that successfully appeals to a Motivation raises Interest. Each Motivation can be successfully appealed to only **once** per Negotiation; after that, future arguments touching the same Motivation are treated as ordinary arguments.

A **Pitfall** is something the NPC dislikes, distrusts, or finds offensive. An argument that touches on a Pitfall fails automatically and damages the Negotiation, no matter how well it’s delivered.

One NPC’s Motivation may be another NPC’s Pitfall. Doing your homework before a Negotiation through research, gossip, or spying is often worth the time.

### Uncovering Motivations and Pitfalls

The most direct way to learn what an NPC wants is to ask. The NPC may volunteer a Motivation freely, especially if they think it will get them a better deal.

If they don’t, or if you’d rather know what to avoid, you can read the NPC by making an **Empathy Test** during the Negotiation.

| Result       | Outcome                                                                                          |
| ------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| 11 or lower  | The NPC notices you trying to read them and becomes annoyed.<br><br>Patience decreases by **1**. |
| 12–16        | You learn nothing.                                                                               |
| 17 or higher | You learn one Motivation **or** one Pitfall — your choice.                                       |

Once you’ve attempted to read the NPC, you can’t try again until your party has made an argument. You can also uncover Motivations and Pitfalls outside the Negotiation entirely — through research, conversations with other NPCs, or by observing the NPC in their daily life.

### Making an Argument

An argument is a single attempt to move the Negotiation in your favor. It can take many forms: a quid-pro-quo offer (“We’ll clear the bandits from your roads if you lend us your guards”), an appeal to an ideal (“A man of honor cannot stand idle while his people suffer”), or an appeal to the NPC’s self-interest (“If the Demon King reaches your gates, your throne won’t be worth the wood it’s made of”).

Every argument needs a **justification** — a reason the NPC should believe what you’re saying. If you skip the justification, the NPC may ask you to back it up before the argument is resolved (“And why should I believe that?”).

Players are encouraged to discuss strategy out of character before committing to an argument. Once you’ve settled on what to say, one player delivers the argument and makes an **Expression Test**.

### Argument Outcomes

The result of an argument depends on whether it touches a Motivation, a Pitfall, or neither, and on the result of the Expression Test.

#### Appealing to a Motivation

| Result | Outcome |
| --- | --- |
| 11 or lower | Patience −1. |
| 12–16 | Interest +1, Patience −1. |
| 17 or higher | Interest +1. |
If the argument is exceptionally well-crafted and well-delivered, the DM may rule that it succeeds without a roll. Strong roleplay should be rewarded.

#### A Neutral Argument

If the argument doesn’t touch a Motivation or a Pitfall, use this table instead.

| Result | Outcome |
| --- | --- |
| 11 or lower | Interest −1, Patience −1. |
| 12–16 | Patience −1. |
| 17 or higher | Interest +1, Patience −1. |
| Natural 20 | Interest +1. Patience does not decrease. |
#### Stepping on a Pitfall

If your argument touches a Pitfall, the test isn’t rolled — the argument fails automatically. Interest drops by **1** and Patience drops by **1**. The NPC will usually warn you not to bring the topic up again.

#### Repeating Yourself

If you make essentially the same argument the NPC has already heard, the test isn’t rolled. The argument fails: Interest −1, Patience −1.

#### Lying

If a player lies to an NPC with an argument that fails to increase the NPC’s interest, the DM can decide that the NPC catches the lie and is offended by it. The NPC’s interest decreases by 1, in addition to any decrease imposed by the failure.

### The NPC’s Response

After every argument, the NPC responds in character. A rise in Interest should be met with warmth or genuine consideration; a drop should be met with disdain, anger, or disappointment; no change should be met with boredom or impatience. The NPC’s reaction is your main signal that an argument hit a Motivation, missed entirely, or stumbled into a Pitfall.

### Final Offers

When the Negotiation ends — whether because Patience reached 1, Interest reached 1 or 6, or the players chose to stop — the NPC’s response is determined by their current Interest.

| Interest          | Response                                                                                                     |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **6** — Yes, and. | The NPC agrees to your original offer, and sweetens the deal in your favor.                                  |
| **5** — Yes.      | The NPC agrees to your original offer as proposed.                                                           |
| **4** — Yes, but. | The NPC agrees, but expects you to sweeten the deal — a favor, a payment, or a concession on your part.      |
| **3** — No, but.  | The NPC refuses your original offer but proposes something smaller as a consolation.                         |
| **2** — No.       | The NPC refuses outright, with no counter-offer.                                                             |
| **1** — No, and.  | The NPC refuses and is now actively hostile to the party. They will work against you unless you make amends. |
### Continuing or Walking Away

If Patience is still above 1 and Interest is between 2 and 5 after the NPC makes an offer, the players can choose to keep negotiating in the hope of a better deal, or accept what’s been offered and end the Negotiation. The players may also end the Negotiation themselves at any time, regardless of where Interest and Patience stand.

Once Patience reaches 1 or Interest reaches 1 or 6, the Negotiation is over — the NPC has nothing more to say.

### Time Management

There's no hard rule on how much time a Negotiation consumes. Consider the circumstances under which the Negotiation takes place. Are pleasantries exchanged? Does it happen during a meal? Does it happen all at once or are there breaks?

Using the example with the local lord, that Negotiation would probably consume an activity time slot. The lord would want time to get to know the players, weigh the options, and discuss the offer with their advisors. On the other hand, negotiating the surrender of a bandit chief might only take a few minutes.
